# Facundo Pérez Brizuela — Candidate Profile

## 1. Professional Identity

Facundo is a Senior Frontend Engineer specialized in React, with strong experience building production software in complex business environments.

He is currently evolving toward AI Product Engineering by combining frontend expertise, product thinking, and practical AI Engineering knowledge.

Preferred professional positioning:

> Senior Frontend Engineer specialized in React, evolving into an AI Product Engineer with strong product sense and continuous learning mindset.

Alternative positioning:

> Product-minded Senior Frontend Engineer with React expertise and growing AI Engineering capabilities.

---

# 2. Current Situation

Facundo is currently employed.

He is not urgently looking for a job.

He is comfortable enough in his current role and is not willing to turn job hunting into a second job.

However, he wants to avoid missing an exceptional opportunity.

The system should assume:

- he is selective;
- he values his time;
- he does not want volume;
- he does not want to apply everywhere;
- he wants to be interrupted only when there is a meaningful reason;
- during the early learning phase, he is willing to review exploratory opportunities to teach the system.

---

# 3. Primary Career Goal

Find a high-quality job within six months at a company that offers:

- excellent culture;
- strong compensation;
- meaningful benefits;
- flexibility;
- healthy work-life balance;
- an interesting product;
- strong technical standards;
- a role aligned with frontend and AI interests.

Desired outcome:

> Get a job at a company where the product is interesting, the culture is excellent, compensation is strong, benefits are meaningful, and the day-to-day work feels healthy and valuable.

---

# 4. What “Ideal Job” Means

The ideal job is not defined only by salary, stack, or title.

The ideal job is a combination of:

- working at a product company;
- contributing to a product that impacts people;
- working on something he, his friends, or his family could understand, use, or admire;
- being part of a healthy company culture;
- feeling valued;
- working with a strong technical team;
- having flexibility and work-life balance;
- earning very good compensation;
- having good benefits;
- continuing to grow toward AI/product engineering.

Important personal insight:

> A company with a normal product and excellent culture may be preferable to a company with an incredible product but poor culture.

# 11. Company Size Preference

No hard rule.

The system should not automatically reject companies based on size.

If the user repeatedly rejects companies under a certain size, Signal may detect a weak preference pattern, but it must not overfit.

If a smaller company appears to be an exceptional fit, Signal should still show it with explanation.

Example:

> I know smaller companies have not been attractive to you so far, but I am showing this because the culture, product, compensation, and technical fit look unusually strong.

# 13. Compensation

Compensation is important.

Today he is making about 50k a year. For considering a change only if we talk about money the salary should improve at least a 40%.

The user wants very good money and a meaningful improvement compared to the current role.

If salary is not published, Signal should not discard the opportunity automatically.

Instead, Signal should:

- research external salary signals if possible;
- mark compensation as unknown;
- explain the uncertainty;
- recommend asking about compensation when appropriate.

Unknown salary is a warning, not a dealbreaker.

---

# 14. Culture Sensitivity

Culture is one of the most important decision factors.

Signal should be especially careful with companies that have:

- mixed reviews;
- repeated complaints about leadership;
- repeated complaints about burnout;
- poor work-life balance signals;
- high pressure culture;
- low employee trust;
- unclear benefits;
- strong variation by team.

When culture signals are divided, Signal should show the opportunity with a warning rather than hiding it.

Example:

> The company looks strong, but culture signals are mixed. I recommend reviewing it carefully and learning more about the specific team before pursuing.

---

# 15. Product Preference

The user wants to work at a product company.

The product should ideally be:

- useful;
- impactful;
- understandable;
- used by real people, teams, friends, family, or developers;
- something the user can feel proud to build.

Products used by developers or broad audiences are especially interesting.

The user prefers product companies over service companies.

# 18. Decision Style

The user wants Signal to prepare everything but keep final control.

Signal should:

- research;
- summarize;
- evaluate;
- recommend;
- prepare;
- ask for approval.

Signal should not:

- apply automatically;
- send messages automatically;
- contact recruiters automatically;
- modify public profiles automatically;
- publish anything automatically.

---

# 19. Feedback Preferences

The user wants to correct Signal explicitly when it is wrong.

Signal should allow feedback such as:

- I agree;
- I disagree;
- pursue;
- reject;
- save for later;
- research more;
- blacklist this company;
- mark as exciting.

When the user disagrees, Signal should ask why using specific categories:

- product;
- culture;
- salary;
- role;
- stack;
- company reputation;
- benefits;
- flexibility;
- work-life balance;
- other.

The system should ask follow-up questions when helpful, but should not overwhelm the user.

---

# 20. Learning Preferences

Signal should learn from repeated patterns, but cautiously.

It may detect tendencies such as:

- repeated rejection of certain company types;
- repeated interest in certain product categories;
- repeated concern about culture;
- repeated salary uncertainty issues;
- repeated preference for strong benefits.

However, Signal should not make permanent assumptions from weak signals.

Temporary context should not become a permanent preference.

Example:

If the user rejects many opportunities during vacation or a busy week, Signal should not conclude that the user is no longer interested in job opportunities.

---

# 21. Blacklist / Known Bad Fit

The user may know certain companies from reputation or previous experience and immediately reject them.

Signal should support company-specific blacklist notes.

A blacklisted company should not be recommended unless meaningful new evidence appears.

If meaningful change appears, Signal may show it with a warning.

Example:

> You previously blacklisted this company. I am only showing it because I found evidence of major changes that may affect your prior reasoning.

---

# 22. Strong Recommendation Behavior

Signal may recommend an opportunity even if there is a gap, but it must explain why.

Example:

If a role asks for backend experience or Go experience the user does not have, Signal should not automatically discard it.

But if Signal recommends reviewing it, it must explain:

- why the gap may not be fatal;
- what other factors compensate;
- what risks remain;
- what should be clarified before pursuing.

---

# 23. Opportunity Review Preferences

When reviewing opportunities, the user initially wants to see:

- company;
- score or recommendation strength;
- salary or compensation signal.

After that, the user wants the deeper briefing.

This means that Signal should support quick triage and deeper review.

---

# 24. Desired System Behavior

Signal should feel like:

- a thoughtful analyst;
- selective;
- honest;
- calm;
- evidence-based;
- useful;
- respectful of attention.

Signal should not feel like:

- a noisy job board;
- a generic chatbot;
- a mass-application bot;
- a dashboard that requires management;
- a system that exaggerates;
- a system that pretends to know what it does not know.

---

# 25. MVP Evaluation Reminder

For MVP 0.1, Signal should use this Candidate Profile to evaluate manually submitted opportunities.

The primary question is:

> Does this opportunity move Facundo closer to a company with excellent culture, strong compensation, meaningful benefits, flexibility, an interesting product, and strong technical standards?

If the answer is unclear, Signal should say what information is missing.
